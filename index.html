Current engines are fundamentally inefficient due to the crankshaft mechanism, which means they only hit their power sweet spot for a fleeting moment. This forces the engine to waste over half the fuel just to maintain power, accelerating the climate crisis due to inefficient burning.

‚öôÔ∏è Mechanism: The Six-Stroke Cycle

A six-stroke hydrogen-powered CV engine operates on an advanced cycle designed to extract maximum efficiency and extremely clean performance from hydrogen fuel.

  1. Stroke 1 (Intake): A fresh charge of hydrogen mixed with air enters the cylinder.
  2. Stroke 2 (Compression): The mixture is compressed as the piston moves upward, preparing it for ignition.
  3. Stroke 3 (First Power): A spark plug ignites the compressed mixture, creating a powerful combustion event that forces the piston downward.
  4. Stroke 4 (Air Injection & Compression): A specially designed port opens, allowing a high-pressure stream of air (compressed earlier by a turbocharger) to enter the cylinder. The piston moves upward, compressing the newly added air and creating ideal conditions for a second power stroke.
  5. Stroke 5 (Second Power): The spark plug ignites the compressed mixture once more, pushing the piston down with an additional burst of energy that significantly boosts overall efficiency.
  6. Stroke 6 (Exhaust): The exhaust gases produced from both combustion cycles are expelled from the cylinder, clearing it for the next cycle.

Unlike conventional engines where all cylinders connect to a crankshaft, this engine uses a special rod assembly that synchronises the six horizontally placed cylinders. This design reduces mechanical losses, improves balance, and delivers smoother power.

Emissions

Because the engine uses hydrogen, it produces virtually zero carbon emissions. The main by-product is nitrogen oxide (NOx), which can be easily converted into harmless nitrogen by a catalytic converter.
